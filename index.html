Competitions
UCLA Data Fest
Ticketmaster.com
2016
After being given data on customers, my team and I set off to find the best reccomendation we could give Ticketmaster for boosting revenue. Our first attempt was by partitioning web traffic data over time by location. We then constructed a model that took location and time as input and returned a coefficient that would be used to modify price. Our intuition was that the more peak the hour, the more money people would be willing to pay. The inspiration for this model came from airline companies that use time-dependent pricing models. We also approached the problem by clustering customers by musical interest and location. With this data, we were able to predict what concerts a customer would be interested in, allowing for a more accurate ad campaign.

Microsoft Hackathon
App development
2015
My team and I came up with the problem of ambulance routing and how to optimize stationing location. We thought that there must be a better way for ambulances to station themselves than sitting in any parking lots they chose. Our attempt at a solution was to cluster patient pick up locations using the K means algorithm, and place an ambulance in the center of this cluster. With any number of ambulances, we could generate a unique location for each car that would decrease average response time.

UCLA Data Fest
Edmunds.com
2015
Our goal with the dataset was to help Edmunds make better decisions about its customers, specifically in their ad campaign. We decided to extrapolate data into a new variable which we coined click density. The idea was that the more frequent the clicks about a certain product over a given amount of time the more interested a customer would likely be. Using this new variable, we created a threshold for ad campaigning: if a customer had a density over x, they were very likely to buy the product so start sending ads to customers with density below x.
